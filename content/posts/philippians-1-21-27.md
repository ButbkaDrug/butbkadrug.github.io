## Intro.
In our scripture for today, Paul continues to explain his situation to the believers in Philippi. If you are new here, or for everyone who maybe missed the last couple of Sundays, I'll remind you that we are going through the letter to the Philippians. Apostle Paul is in a Roman prison. He sends a letter to his friends to say thank you and encourage them.

Christians in Philippi loved Paul very much. He was an important figure in their lives. He started their church. He was their spiritual father.

They were very concerned about him and his situation. It had been going on for years at this point. And they knew that it could resolve in two ways.

1. The first option is what they desired and prayed for: Paul's release.

2. The second option was not so appealing. It was Paul's execution.

### Project to our life
Can you imagine something like that happening to your friend? To someone who is very dear to your heart?

I think you would also be very concerned. You would want this person to be released. For all accusations to be dropped.

We would not spare our resources to help that person. To speak for them. Pray for them. Provide financial support, etc.

And there is nothing wrong with that. I believe that this is the proper attitude. You **should** feel this way and react this way.

You know, if your friend is detained under false accusations, potentially facing the death sentence, and it doesn't bother you at all... I mean, don't get me wrong, but I think there is something wrong with you then.

**It should bother you!** You should lose sleep over it. It should occupy your mind.

And especially if this person is a committed believer or a pastor, missionary, etc., we can start having questions: **Where is God?** **Why doesn't He intervene in the situation?**

We can get discouraged in our faith if the situation does not quickly resolve. It can be a challenge for our faith. Isn't it?

### Recent Situation
We've had a remotely similar situation just recently. Our dear brother got detained by the immigration service and ended up in a so-called "immigration prison," even though he did everything possible on his side to follow the law.

And you know what? **We were** concerned about him. It did not feel right. It didn't feel like it was God's will for him. At least to me it didn't.

Our brother was a good Christian. A faithful minister. He served the Lord here in Batumi. He ministered to an unreached people group, sharing the gospel with them. And it was pretty successful. So logically, one would think that nothing like that should ever happen to him. Right?

### When it happens
And indeed, when things like that happen, it is confusing. It almost immediately raises the question: **Where is God?** **Why doesn't He help His faithful minister?** **Has God's plan failed?** **Was God defeated?**

Because in our minds **obviously it cannot be God's will...** for him.

**Or can it?**

If such feelings are not foreign to you, if such questions have gone through your mind before, I have good news. Our today's scripture will shed some light on what might be happening behind the scenes. We will see whether it is something that we should be concerned about. And maybe it will give us some clues about what we should do in our current situation with the ever-evolving complications of staying here.

## INRO END

### History
And in order to understand Paul's situation better, we should go back to the book of Acts. We will look at the end of the third missionary journey of Apostle Paul, at the point in time when things started to develop.

Let's open **Acts 19** and jump to the verses where Paul and his companions accomplished the goal of their journey and started to think about what comes next. In verse 21 Apostle Luke writes the following:

### Acts 19:21
> ²¹Now after these things were finished, Paul resolved in the Spirit to go to Jerusalem after he had passed through Macedonia( which almost sertanly means through the church in Phillipy) and Achaia, saying, “After I have been there, I must also see Rome.”

### Personal conviction
In these verses, we can see that Paul and the team (and from now on I'll mostly refer to their crew as "Paul," but keep in mind he was not alone) accomplished the goal of their third missionary journey. And naturally they came to a point where they needed to decide what was next. This trip was finished. They did all they wanted to do. So what do we do now?

Our text says, "after these things were finished **Paul resolved in the Spirit**." Or as other translations say, "**He felt compelled by the Spirit...**".

Now, in the New Testament, when the Bible talks about the "Holy Spirit" and, let's say, the "spirit of man," it uses the same word **πνεῦμα (pneuma)**. Usually the context is the key to understanding whether we are talking about the Holy Spirit of God or the human spirit. Some authors add the words *Holy Spirit*, but others don't.

So in this situation we have two possibilities, two general opinions:

1. The idea to go to Jerusalem came from the Holy Spirit, or at least it was approved by the Holy Spirit.  
2. It was just Paul's idea.

And honestly, as far as our study for today goes, it is almost irrelevant which one is more true. It's not that important for today.

But what **is** important is to understand that the trip to Jerusalem, whether it was a mistake on Paul's side or not, and all the trouble Paul got into, were not the result of his sin or anything else. Everything that happened to him there was **ONLY** because of his faith in Jesus Christ.

It was not because he didn't pay his taxes. Or because he was smuggling something. Or I don't know... breaking some laws. No. All the persecution and accusations, all the hate, were only because of his faith in Jesus. Because of his mission to preach salvation to the Gentiles.

### The Prophet
And as they continued toward Jerusalem, the Holy Spirit kept speaking about what would happen. So Paul did not go unaware. Quite the opposite. In Acts 20 verses 22 and 23 we read:

### Acts 20:22,23
²²And now, behold, bound by the Spirit, I am on my way to Jerusalem, not knowing what will happen to me there,²³except that the Holy Spirit solemnly testifies to me in every city, saying that chains and afflictions await me.

And later in chapter 21, when they had already reached Tyre, Luke writes:

### Acts 21:4
⁴After looking up the disciples, we stayed there for seven days; and they kept telling Paul, through the Spirit, not to set foot in Jerusalem.

And at last, when they got even closer to Jerusalem, we read about a prophet coming down from Judea in verses 10 and 11 of the same chapter:

### Acts 21:10-11
> ¹⁰...a prophet named Agabus came down from Judea.¹¹And he came to us and took Paul’s belt and bound his own feet and hands, and said, “This is what the Holy Spirit says: ‘In this way the Jews in Jerusalem will bind the man who owns this belt and hand him over to the Gentiles.’

God was speaking a lot about what would happen. I think it was important both for Paul and for other people to hear this. His companions and believers in the churches they visited were informed by the Holy Spirit. At this point everybody knew what was about to happen.

Some people say that Paul was in direct rebellion based on verse 4 of chapter 21. Others tend to think that it was just an interpretation of what the Holy Spirit was saying rather than Paul's rebellion. But again, it is not that important. We will try to look at both possibilities.

I think that through these prophecies we can see that God is in control. At this point it was very clear that Paul would get arrested. That bad things would happen to him if he set foot in the city. No one had any doubt about it.

And when people hear this, they have a typical, very normal, very human reaction. Let's continue reading in verse 12:

### Acts 21:12-14
> ”¹²When we had heard this, we as well as the local residents *began* begging him not to go up to Jerusalem.¹³Then Paul replied, “What are you doing, weeping and breaking my heart? For I am ready not only to be bound, but even to die in Jerusalem for the name of the Lord Jesus.”

> ¹⁴And since he would not be persuaded, we became quiet, remarking, “The will of the Lord be done!”

We can see that Paul made a choice. A very conscious, very informed choice. He knew ahead of time what would happen. And no one would judge him if he decided not to go. In fact, as we can see, people begged him not to go. Not only regular believers, but also Apostle Luke and Timothy, his companions, and some other mighty men of God who had been through rough times together with Paul.

### Observation
When we read other places in Acts, we can see that a few times the Holy Spirit did not allow Paul to go to certain places—and Paul didn't. This is how they went to Europe, to Philippi, instead of Asia Minor as Paul had planned.

This time the situation is different. God warns Paul about trouble, but I think He leaves the choice up to Paul—whether to go or not.

And Paul already expressed his position in verse 13.

So I think we can agree that Paul's imprisonment was not some kind of mistake. Not an error or a result of confusion. It was a choice that Paul made. A path that he deliberately picked.

And God supported him in that. In chapter 23 verse 11:

### Acts 23:11
> ¹¹But on the following night, the Lord stood near him and said, “Be courageous! For as you have testified to the *truth* about Me in Jerusalem, so you must testify in Rome also.”

### With Paul
So whether Paul rebelled, as some argue, or not, we see that God was with Paul. He encourages Paul, and these words should encourage us as well. Even when you find yourself in such a complicated situation, God is still in control.

Knowing the end of the story, we know that because of his imprisonment Paul was able to testify, to witness, to share the gospel with several kings—people of the highest rank in the Roman Empire. And in the end, with the emperor of Rome himself. Isn't that remarkable?

God was able to yield some remarkable outcomes from this very intense, very difficult situation. He was in control. He is the winner. And as for Paul, he was not only ready, but happy to do those things, as we will shortly see.

### Back to Philippians
But as we come back to the book of Philippians, they didn't know what the end would be. In fact, all they knew was that Paul was in prison. Maybe they had heard about his hardships on the way to Rome.

From a human point of view, Paul's imprisonment might look like a defeat. As if God's plan failed. As if something went wrong. Such a powerful minister now in prison.

People were concerned and worried. And rightfully so, considering all the things Paul went through.

That's why in his letter Paul tells the Philippians not to be discouraged because of his imprisonment. We read the words "joy" and "rejoice" many times throughout the letter, which tells us Paul is not depressed about his situation. He embraced his choice.

And honestly, it doesn't sound like he is desperate for relief. In Philippians 1:19 Paul talks about his deliverance. But in verse 20 we see that *deliverance* for him could mean both **LIFE** as well as **DEATH**. Both options are viable for him. Even more so, he says that the second one is actually more desirable.

### Philippians 1:18-21
> ...I also will rejoice,¹⁹for I know that this will turn out for my deliverance through your prayers and the provision of the Spirit of Jesus Christ,²⁰according to my eager expectation and hope, that I will not be put to shame in anything, but *that* with all boldness, Christ will even now, as always, be exalted in my body, whether by **life** or by **death**.


Unlike many of us, Paul was not afraid of difficult circumstances, or even death. When we get into hardships, all we want is to get relief. We want our lives to get back to a normal state. And I think the same was the position of the Philippian church. They wanted Paul to go back to his ministry. That's what their prayers were about. But Paul's attitude is a bit different. In verse 21 he says:

### Philippians 1:21-30
> ²¹For to me, to live is Christ, and to die is gain.

He is letting us know that he will be happy either way. Last Sunday we read that at this point everybody knew that Paul was in chains *because of Christ*, or *for the sake of Christ*. Which means that everyone knew he was not some kind of criminal. That Paul did nothing wrong. Everyone knew that **he was there for Christ.**

Paul comforts his friends, saying: "Don't worry about me. Everyone knows I'm innocent. I'm neither a rebel nor a criminal. Everybody knows it's about Christ. And no matter how it will all turn out for me, it will all serve for the glory of God."

And further, Paul continues to explain his attitude toward life in general:

### Philippians 1:22-30
> ²²But if *I am* to live *on* in the flesh, this *will mean* fruitful labor for me; and I do not know which to choose.²³But I am hard-pressed from both *directions,* having the desire to depart and be with Christ, for *that* is very much better;²⁴yet to remain on in the flesh is more necessary for your sakes.²⁵Convinced of this, I know that I will remain and continue with you all for your progress and joy in the faith,²⁶so that your pride in Christ Jesus may be abundant because of me by my coming to you again.

Paul says that **to live in the flesh means to continue his work for Christ**. He is not looking forward to some sort of retirement. When he would be able to travel around and see the world. When he would finally settle down somewhere on a distant farm and live for himself a little bit. No. He says that if he is to stay, he will surely continue with his ministry. He is not discouraged by his imprisonment. He is not terrified by the possibility of execution. He embraced that and is ready to continue.

We do not read even the slightest hint of regret. No. Paul states that he will continue to do exactly what he has been doing. That all this will serve for the glory of God.

### How it affects me
And such a stance amazes me. I'll confess, I don't like trouble. I like when everything goes well. And this is what I'm praying for all the time. But now, as we enter a time of uncertainty—when laws in the country are changing and it's not clear how life will be in a couple of months—this gives me courage.

Paul was in a far worse situation, and somehow he managed to preserve boldness in Christ. This reminds me that God is in control. And as we deal with minor inconveniences, we can "keep calm and carry on."

The obstacles that we face should not scare us away from doing what God called us to do. From being the people God wants us to be. We can be like Paul. We can have the same boldness too.

And I know that each of us is going through some hardships right now. We all have our own battles and struggles. And the encouragement for us is that God is in control.

And as we read today, even if you are the sole reason for your struggles, God is not going to abandon you in the midst of them. He is still able to help you. And willing to help you.

### Boldness like Paul
But if you want to have Paul's kind of boldness, or at least start moving in that direction, I have a few useful, hopefully practical tips for you. I think these will be especially useful reminders in our current obstacles as foreigners. And these are the most common things that I see getting people into trouble:

1. We should be as much as possible compliant with local laws.

    * This will ensure that we are not bringing trouble on ourselves by being foolish or trying to be too clever. That if we are to suffer, it would surely be for Christ's sake alone, and not because we were trying to cheat the system. We need to be sure that no one is able to accuse us of anything else but our faith in Christ Jesus. Because if you have some shady things in your life, be sure the enemy will bring them up to compromise you. By making the decision to break the law or by trying to "be clever," you only invite trouble into your life—and you disobey Scripture as well. Be proactive about this.

2. Know your battlefield

    * Both Jesus and Paul lived at a time when their homeland was occupied. They lived under the burden of the Roman Empire, which was not perfect and not very fair. Yet they knew that their battlefield was not politics. Their mission was not revolution or anything like that. Their mission was to spread the Gospel and help people. This is what concerned them. And I know this may rub some people the wrong way, but I'll say it anyway. I believe the same applies to us. Our first concern should be the Kingdom of God. We should pray for and bless the authorities. We should obey them, as long as it does not break God's law. We should remember that first of all we are citizens of the Kingdom of God, and all our efforts should go in that direction.

3. The most important I saved for the end — Trust God

    * If we truly trust that God is in control, it helps a lot. When you follow His lead, when you are sure that you are where He wants you to be, and when you do the things God wants you to do, you can be sure that He is in control. That He will take care of you as long as you trust in Him.

    * But if you are the lord of your life, and you are the one who decides everything, if you live by your own wisdom, then... well... I don't know.
