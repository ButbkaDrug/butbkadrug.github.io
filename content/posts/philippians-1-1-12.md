### Intro
Last time we had an overview of our new book, the letter to the Philippians. We looked at Paul’s journey that preceded this letter. We learned about Paul’s situation and the history not only of the city of Philippi, but also of the first church on the European continent.

### Recap
As a recap from last time, in case you missed it: Paul is under house arrest in Rome, waiting for his court appearance before Caesar.

The church in Philippi collects financial support for Paul and sends it with a trustworthy member of the church.

The letter to the Philippians—the one we are about to study—is Paul’s response that he sends back to the church after receiving their support.

### Uniqeness of the letter
Unlike other letters of Paul, the letter to the Philippians is not meant to correct or rebuke. It does not confront false teachings. Nor is it a letter defending the gospel or Paul’s apostleship. It does not address any major issue in the church.

This letter is different. It is more about affirmation, gratitude, unity, and humility. But even though it does not go deeply into theology, there is still a lot for us to learn.

Last time we did a deep dive into the historical context of the book. And today I invite you to start reading and exploring the text:

### Philippians 1:1,2
> ¹Paul and Timothy, bond-servants of Christ Jesus, 

> To all the saints in Christ Jesus who are in Philippi, including the overseers and deacons:²Grace to you and peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ.

### First Break
Let’s stop here and make some observations. What do we see in this text?

We can call this section of the text a “header” or “greetings,” maybe even an “introduction” before the actual letter.

I understand that in modern times most people don’t write letters. They are still used in the business world. But as far as personal communication goes, we have moved on to all kinds of messaging apps instead. So this kind of structure might feel foreign to a modern reader.

Typically, no one bothers writing emails, not to mention handwritten letters. It is a forgotten art in some sense. I think some of us can still recall how it was done. But the younger generation might have never written one of those.

### Did you even write a letter?
Raise your hand if you have never written or don’t remember writing and sending a physical letter.

So, for the younger folks, this is how you start a letter. You begin with greetings and by introducing yourself.

Simple things that tell the recipient who wrote the letter—things like **your name** and maybe a brief summary of **who you are** and your relationship to the recipient.

It is a little bit like messaging someone who doesn’t know you, or someone who doesn’t have you in their contact list.

### Example
For example, my mom was not careful on Telegram, and some scammers hijacked her account. They locked her out and started spamming all her friends. It happened a while ago. Basically, she completely lost access to her Telegram account.

When I was visiting her recently, I tried to help. Apparently, it’s not that easy. To make a long story short, in the end we had to delete her account entirely and recreate it a few days later.

After it was all done—with some security enhancements this time—she had to do this very thing: message her friends and family.

She would write messages like:

"Hey Dasha, it's Mom!"
or  
"Hi Valya, it is Grandma."

She would use words like *Mom*, *Grandma*, *classmate*, and so on—different “roles” or “titles” to help people understand who the message was from. At the same time, it sets the tone and the framework of the conversation. It tweaks the reader’s perception of the text.

We all do it from time to time, don’t we?

### Back to Paul and Timothy
Same with Paul's letter. He writes:

### Philippians 1:1
> ¹Paul and Timothy, bond-servants of Christ Jesus...

We can observe that Paul not only mentions himself, but also Timothy. Timothy was Paul’s companion when they started the church in Philippi. The people in the church knew Timothy almost as well as they knew Paul. It also lets them know that they are still working together after ten years of ministry.

### Bond Servants
Next, what we see in the text is Paul saying “bond-servants.” In Greek it would be [ doulos ], which literally means “slaves.”

...

Interesting… *Paul and Timothy*—slaves?

Why, out of all possible ways to describe themselves, does Paul choose this one?

One might think it’s not important—that it’s just a figure of speech, a metaphor, or some literary technique. But I can assure you it is not. I’m convinced it was a very deliberate choice. **Paul is making an important statement.**

He chooses the lowest rank in Roman society and associates himself with it. Slaves were considered more property than people. They had no rights, no part in civic life, and no respect. There was an unfathomable gap between Roman citizens and slaves. Roman citizens were proud of their status. Pride was a big part of society, and it carried over into the church as well.

**Paul sets the example of humility.** Disregarding his status as an apostle, a Roman citizen, and a Jewish rabbi, he associates himself with the lowest rank possible—slaves.

In other words, Paul is saying: “Hey, it’s me and Timothy, and we consider ourselves nothing more than slaves.”

### Paul and Timothy
Another interesting observation is that he writes **Paul and Timothy, bond-servants**… And again, this is a bit different from his other letters. If we look at Second Corinthians, we see:

### 2 Corinthians 1:1
> ¹Paul, an apostle of Christ Jesus by the will of God, and *our* brother Timothy…

See the difference compared with the letter to the Philippians?

In the letter to Corinth, you can clearly see a big difference between who Paul is and who Timothy is.

In the letter to the Philippians, Paul puts both himself and Timothy on the same level of authority. Not by accident. Not because he was in a good mood or something. This was intentional.

Paul was highly regarded in the church. People looked up to him as an example, with great respect and honor. Both he and Timothy started the church. Most likely, Paul appointed the elders and deacons. They knew about Paul’s ministry for the Lord. He was an outstanding person, and they knew it.

By putting himself and Timothy on the same level, Paul lowers himself in the eyes of the church. Because Paul and Timothy were not at the same level of authority. Also, most likely Timothy was not a Roman citizen, which makes this statement even more radical for the listeners—especially when Paul associates both of them with slaves.

**Paul sets the example of humility.**

### Overseers And Deacons
Let's move further... Furthur we read:

### Philippians 1:1-2
> ...To all the saints in Christ Jesus who are in Philippi, including the overseers and deacons:²Grace to you and peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ.

This section looks like the standard greeting Paul uses in his letters—except for one thing. It was bugging me from the first time I read this line. I kept looking at the text, reading it again and again, with the question in my head:

- Why does he explicitly mention "overseers and deacons"?

He doesn’t do anything like that in his other letters. So why here?

### Answer
And the answer is quite simple. At this point, Paul was aware of issues in the church. Anisimus, the guy who brought the money, gave him a heads-up. Paul was making sure that the overseers and deacons would pay attention to his words, just like everyone else. 

He wanted them not to think of themselves as those who had already reached fullness. These instructions were not for them alone, but for regular Christians—mere mortals.

That is why Paul specifically calls them out, saying: "Overseers and deacons, please, listen—I’m speaking to you as well."

### Affirmation
Paul continues with some affirmation:

### Philippians 1:3-6
> ³I thank my God in all my remembrance of you,⁴always offering prayer with joy in my every prayer for you all,⁵in view of your participation in the gospel from the first day until now.

>⁶*For I am* confident of this very thing, that He who began a good work among you will complete it by the day of Christ Jesus.

In verses 3-5, he praises the Philippians for their faithful participation in the gospel. I read this as showing that the Philippians were doers—faithful followers and workers who applied the Word in their lives and promoted the gospel further. They had no problems with that.

Verse 6 serves both as encouragement and a reminder. On one hand, it reminds us that the work is not finished yet—that no one has reached completion. We are works in progress.

On the other hand, it is an encouragement. God will continue to work in us until the day of Christ Jesus. He is not going to give up or discard anyone. So even if you do not advance as quickly as you think you should in your walk with the Lord, or if you fall every now and then, it does not mean that God will give up on you. He will continue the work He started until the Day of Christ.

And lastly, verse 6 serves as a reminder that it is God who is doing the work. It’s not about you, or how good you are relative to other people. It is not your achievement. It is God—it is His work in you.

### Application
How does it all apply to us? Well, if we take a sober look, there is not too much difference between us and the Philippians.

- We both live under a New Covenant, which removes any doubt about whether this truth applies to us.  
- We also currently live in a shame- and pride-based culture, just like in Rome.  
- Our church also gathers people from different social layers, with different levels of financial well-being.  
- And more.

### Conclusion
What are your areas of pride? People are extremely susceptible to pride, and the enemy uses it all the time. What makes you feel like you are better than others?

Maybe you feel like you’ve already figured it out…



You know: I to church for a decades at this point. I don’t smoke. I don’t drink. I don’t live a loose sexual life. I don’t break laws. I don’t know… I’m all good, really.

Or maybe the other way around.

You feel like a failure—that you are so difficult and stubborn that God will give up on you, if He hasn’t already…

Both are lies. We are *works in progress*. Not yet finished, but we will be finished.

The work will be completed only on the day of Jesus Christ.
